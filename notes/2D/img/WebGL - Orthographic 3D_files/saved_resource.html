                          greggman Mod AT • 2 years ago • edited

                          I'm not sure what you mean by "default camera". WebGL doesn't have a camera. Camera is something you, the programmer supply by adding math to your shaders.

                          If you use no math (so I guess that's the default) and you draw things in clip space then z = -1 is closer than z = +1.

                          https://jsfiddle.net/greggm...

                          To be even more pedentic, even that is arbitrary. The z-buffer is just an array of depth values used to test the Z value of a pixel about to be draw with Z-values of pixels previously drawn. What test to use is settable. The default is LESS meaning that if the new pixel's Z-value is less than the value in the z-buffer then draw the new pixels. -1 is less than +1 so that's why we get the results that work above. But you can change that test by calling gl.depthTest to for example GREATER and get the opposite results.

                          I'm mostly writing that to just trying to emphasize that WebGL is really just a rasteration engine and everything related to 3D, Cameras, objects, etc are all programmer constructions.

                                        greggman Mod jahbLAI4D • 3 years ago • edited

                                        Probably because it's the most generic and provides adequate speed. Most people choose the generic solution first and then optimize later if and only if the generic solution is too slow. For most use cases the generic solution is easiest and fast enough. For those cases where it's not fast enough usually the solutions for optimization will provide far more speed than simply switching to 3x3+3. There's also things like SIMD and vectorization which generally work better or at least no slower with 4x4 instead of 3x3+3 because the hardware is designed to work with 4 values at a time in parallel and has no extra overhead for reading/using 4 values vs 3. The same is true of GPUs so I suspect there are few GPUs that run faster with 3x3+3 vs just 4x4.

                                        Of course if you prefer 3x3+3 that's a completely valid choice. Back in the 90s on PCs and PS1s it was common to do that kind of thing. Now-a-days less so. You'll see for example both Unreal and Unity use 4x4 matrices.

                                        Related to 4x4's genericness it's certainly nice to be able to use the inverseWorldViewProjection matrix for picking or ray casting the mouse etc as just one example. I suppose with the right set of functions that still easy with 3x3+3 but I suspect that's an example where it's easier with just generic 4x4 matrix functions.
